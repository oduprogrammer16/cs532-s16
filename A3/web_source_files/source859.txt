<h1 class="mainStoryheadlineprint" style="z-index:99999;">
Assembly committee advances resolution to give voters a say on North Jersey casino expansion
</h1>
<div style="margin-top:10px;"></div>	
<div class="storyauthorv2" style="text-transform:uppercase;font-size:12px;font-weight:bold;">By JOHn BRENNAn</div>		
<div class="storyauthorv2" style="float:left;text-transform:uppercase;font-size:10px;line-height:14px;">staff writer&nbsp;|&nbsp;</div>				
                                      <div class="storyauthorv3" style="float:left;text-transform:uppercase;font-size:10px;line-height:14px;">The Record</div>
<div class="clear"></div>
<div style="padding-bottom:13px;border-bottom:1px #ccc solid;"></div>
										           		                     		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          		          				
		
		
		
		
		
<div style="text-align:left;">
	
<div id="storybody">
			

	
	

	
	
	
    
	<p>
	TRENTON — An Assembly committee on Monday approved a resolution that would allow voters statewide to decide in November whether to permit two new casino licenses to be issued in the northern half of the state.</p>
		
	

	
	
	

    				<div class="storyboxholderv2" style="clear: both;">
						<figure style="margin:0px; padding:0px;">
							<div id="storymedia" class="storyboxbodyv2" style="margin-top:15px;">
								<img title='The New Jersey State House' height='232' alt='The New Jersey State House' width='300' class='left' src='/polopoly_fs/1.1040265.1403556737!/fileImage/httpImage/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/landscape_300/statehouse.jpg' />																	<div class="photographerv2">RECORD FILE PHOTO</div>
																																		<figcaption>
										<div class="photocaptionv2" >
											The New Jersey State House
										</div>
									</figcaption>
															</div>
						</figure>
					</div>
    
	
<p>
	The 6-2 vote by the Assembly Judiciary Committee sets up a vote by the full Assembly, expected in about three weeks, with the state Senate following suit on an identical resolution on March 14, according to state Sen. Paul Sarlo, a Wood-Ridge Democrat who is sponsoring the Senate bill. If 60 percent of each chamber approves, the referendum will go before voters on the Nov. 8 ballot.</p>

	
		
	

	
	
	
    
	
<p>
	Assemblyman Chris A. Brown, R-Atlantic, raised objections to the process. Brown, a member of the committee, said it was premature to vote on the resolution until a tax rate for the North Jersey casinos was determined so officials could estimate how much tax revenue from those facilities would be directed to support redevelopment in Atlantic City.</p>

	
		
	

	
	
	
    
	
<p>
	Both resolutions allow for up to $200 million in new taxes to be designated annually to help Atlantic City transition from its heavy dependence on casinos. The city’s gambling revenues — $2.6 billion in 2015 — have declined by about half in the past decade in the face of competition from casinos in Pennsylvania and New York.</p>

	
		
	

	
	
	
    
	
<p>
	“A lot of time carnival barkers promise a lot, but when you come into the tent, there’s nothing there,” Brown said, insisting that passage of the resolution and the approval by voters of the referendum would “kill Atlantic City.”</p>

			<div class="storyboxholderv2" style="clear: both;">
		<div id="300x250_PRINT">
		<script type="text/javascript">
		googletag.cmd.push(function() { googletag.display("300x250_PRINT"); });
		</script>
		</div>
		</div>
	
		
	

	
	
	
    
	
<p>
	But Assemblyman Ralph Caputo, D-Essex, countered, “You’re spinning your wheels, Chris.”</p>

	
		
	

	
	
	
    
	
<p>
	Caputo, the prime sponsor of the bill, said in testimony before the committee on Monday that “everyone is panicking” over the economic collapse of Atlantic City except Brown — adding that it was worrisome that Brown still seeks to maintain the status quo.</p>

	
		
	

	
	
	
    
	
<p>
	Atlantic City’s casinos pay an effective tax rate of 9.25 percent, compared to more than 50 percent paid by casinos in neighboring states. Sarlo and Stephen Sweeney, D-Gloucester, the state Senate president, have suggested that the</p>

	
		
	

	
	
	
    
	
<p>
	North Jersey casinos might pay taxes in the 25 to 30 percent range, while also saying that the exact amount has not been determined.</p>

	
		
	

	
	
	
    
	
<p>
	Caputo said after the hour-long committee discussion that he will seek to have the tax rate and numerous other details clarified and made public ahead of the November referendum.</p>

	
		
	

	
	
	
    
	
<p>
	Even if voters give the measure the go-ahead, requests for proposals from the operators of existing Atlantic City casinos — who under the resolution would get first crack at the two North Jersey licenses — might not be circulated until mid-2017. So it could be up to five or six years before any North Jersey casino tax revenue starts flowing to Atlantic City to help fund its redevelopment.</p>

	
		
	

	
	
	
    
	
<p>
	Jersey City and the Meadowlands Sports Complex in East Rutherford are widely considered to be the most likely sites for the two billion-dollar North Jersey casinos. However, the resolution states that the new casinos would be built in separate counties at least 72 miles north of Atlantic City, meaning that any location from New Brunswick in Middlesex County on north is a potential candidate.</p>

	
		
	

	
	
	
    
	
<p>
	A spokesman for Freehold Raceway told the committee that the harness track’s owners “would have to think about their continued viability” in the face of added competition for gambling dollars from two new casinos.<br />
	In written testimony to the committee, Gordon MacInnes, president of New Jersey Policy Perspective, a liberal think tank, asked why the resolutions were moving forward so quickly.</p>

	
		
	

	
	
	
    
	
<p>
	“Given New Jersey’s track record with gambling — one of big promises, lack of delivery on those promises, and little long-term economic benefit — one would think there would be more caution and skepticism about expanding casinos to North Jersey,” MacInnes wrote.</p>
