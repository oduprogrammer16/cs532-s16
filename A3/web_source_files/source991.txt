<div id='wsite-content'><div id='blog-post-652499042692238370' class='blog-post'><div class='blog-header'><h2 class='blog-title'><a href='/2/post/2016/02/emdr-processing-traumatic-memories.html' class='blog-title-link' class='blog-link'>EMDR: Processing Traumatic Memories</a></h2> <p class='blog-date'><span class='date-text'>02/08/2016</span></p><!-- 652499042692238370 -->
<p class='blog-comments'><a href='/2/post/2016/02/emdr-processing-traumatic-memories.html#comments' class='blog-link'>0 Comments</a></p></div><div class='blog-separator'> &nbsp; </div><div class='blog-content'><span class='imgPusher' style='float:left;height:0px'></span><span style='z-index:10;position:relative;float:left;;clear:left;margin-top:0px;*margin-top:0px'><a><img src="/uploads/3/9/1/6/3916940/8179551.jpg?486" style="margin-top: 5px; margin-bottom: 10px; margin-left: 0px; margin-right: 10px; border-width:1px;padding:3px;" alt="Picture" class="galleryImageBorder" /></a><span style="display: block; font-size: 90%; margin-top: -10px; margin-bottom: 10px; text-align: center;" class="wsite-caption"></span></span> <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;display:block;">Let's touch on an approach called&nbsp;<em>Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing(EMDR</em><strong><em>)</em></strong>. My psychologist used it to help me convert some traumatic memories into safe thoughts. For example, during one of my psychotic episodes, the voices in my head screamed out vulgar and offensive comments while I was in public places. Since one of my delusions was that people could listen to my thoughts, I assumed that they could also hear the voices. Likewise, I believed that folks presumed I was the one using this abhorrent language. This spawned intense social anxiety that stayed with me long after I recovered. My therapist attacked the situation, using&nbsp;EMDR.&nbsp;Once we completed the process, I was able to store the memory in my brain without the emotions attached to it. Consequently, being in crowds of people became much easier for me.<br /><br />EMDR&nbsp;uses an instrument composed of a wide rectangular box with a row of lights ranging from one end to the next. Mine also had two small pumps that I held in each hand. As I sat in my chair&ndash;holding the pumps and watching the lights&ndash;the lights would blink on and off in sequence from left to right in repetition. It was a bit like watching a pendulum swing back and forth. Meanwhile, the pumps would pulsate from left to right in beat with the lights. As I did this, my therapist asked me to visualize a specific memory. Then she posed questions on the feelings that particular image evoked, asking how much it disturbed me on a scale from one to ten. We repeated the process&shy;&ndash;with the addition of a few added details&ndash;until the memory no longer agitated me. In the end, I focused on a future, more positive version of the same situation.<br /><br />Why does this work? The eye movement affects how the brain processes the information. It seems to allow the mind to store unresolved thoughts into the file in our brain labeled "worked out."&nbsp; Once the thought is re-categorized, it loses its unfavorable power over us.<br /><em><br />To get a more in-depth understanding of EMDR, check out the EMDR International Association website at&nbsp;<a href="http://www.emdria.org/?2" target="_blank" title="">http://www.emdria.org/?2</a></em><br /><br /><strong>You can read about other therapeutic choices on my <a href="/psychotherapy-and-choices.html" title="">Psychotherapy and Choices</a> page.</strong></div> <hr style="width:100%;clear:both;visibility:hidden;"></hr>  <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:center;"><a href="/blog.html"><strong>Return to the top of the page.</strong></a></div></div><div class='blog-social'><div class='blog-social-item'><a class='twitter-share-button' href='http://twitter.com/share?url=http%3A%2F%2Floveskaleidoscope.com%2F2%2Fpost%2F2016%2F02%2Femdr-processing-traumatic-memories.html' data-count='horizontal'></a></div><div class='blog-social-item blog-fb-like'><fb:like href='http://loveskaleidoscope.com/2/post/2016/02/emdr-processing-traumatic-memories.html' show_faces='false' layout='button_count' width='90'></fb:like></div><div style='clear:both'></div></div>
