                                                                <h1 class="title">How Hillary Clinton&#039;s State Department Sold Fracking to the World</h1>
                                                               </div> <!-- /#content-header -->
                
        <div id="content-area" class="clear-block">
          <div id="node-257241" class="node node-type-article node-type-article-full"><div class="node-inner clear-block">

  
  
  
  
  <div class="content clear-block">
    <div id="node-header" class="clear-block"><div id="node-header-data" class="node-header-data-primary"><h3 class="dek">A trove of secret documents details the US government&#039;s global push for shale gas.</h3><p class="byline byline-byline">&mdash;By <a href="/authors/mariah-blake" rel="author">Mariah Blake</a></p><div id="dateline"> | <a href="/toc/2014/09">September/October 2014 Issue</a></div></div><!--node header data--><div class="hdr-tools"><ul class="article-tools"><li class="fblike toolbar-btn"><fb:like href="http://www.motherjones.com/environment/2014/09/hillary-clinton-fracking-shale-state-department-chevron" layout="button_count" action="like" show_faces="true" share="true"></fb:like></li><li class="tweet toolbar-btn"><a href="http://twitter.com/share" class="twitter-share-button" data-related="davidcorndc:Washington editor of Mother Jones magazine and blogger at www.davidcorn.com,kdrum:Political blogger and writer for Mother Jones magazine.,tomphilpott:The cofounder of Maverick Farms and food & agriculture correspondent for Mother Jones.,clarajeffery:Co-editor of Mother Jones. One very tired lady."  data-size="" data-text="How Hillary Clinton's State Department sold fracking to the world" data-url="http://www.motherjones.com/environment/2014/09/hillary-clinton-fracking-shale-state-department-chevron" data-count="none" data-via="motherjones">Tweet</a><script type="text/javascript" src="http://platform.twitter.com/widgets.js"></script></li><li class="forward toolbar-btn top-social-button largebutton genericlargebutton"><a href="/forward?path=node/257241" title="Forward by Email"><div class="btn-o"><div class="btn"><i class="icon-envelope" style="color: cornflowerblue"></i><span class="nearicon">Email</span></div></div></a></li><div class="comment-tool comment-bar nonteaser-comment-bar"><span id="comment-icon" class="comment-container"><span class="comment-number"><a href="http://www.motherjones.com/node/257241#disqus_thread" class="comments disqus-comment-count active" data-disqus-identifier="node/257241" title="Jump to the comments of this posting."></a></span></div><li class="print toolbar-btn"><a href="/print/257241" title="Printer Friendly Version"><i class="icon-print" style="font-size: 20px;padding-top: 0px;"></i></a></li></ul></div></div><div id="node-body-top" class="clear-block"><div class="node-master-image-large"><img src="http://www.motherjones.com/files/imagecache/top-of-content-main/ukraine_a630x354.jpg" alt="" title="" width="630" height="354" class="imagecache imagecache-top-of-content-main"/><span class="master-image-caption"></span> <span class="byline photo-byline">Illustration by John Ritter</span></div><p><span class="section-lead">One icy morning</span> in February 2012, Hillary Clinton's plane touched down in the Bulgarian capital, Sofia, which was just digging out from a fierce blizzard. Wrapped in a thick coat, the secretary of state descended the stairs to the snow-covered tarmac, where she and her aides piled into a motorcade bound for the presidential palace. That afternoon, they huddled with Bulgarian leaders, including Prime Minister Boyko Borissov, discussing everything from Syria's bloody civil war to their joint search for loose nukes. But the focus of the talks was fracking. The previous year, Bulgaria had signed a five-year, $68 million deal, granting US oil giant Chevron millions of acres in shale gas concessions. Bulgarians were outraged. Shortly before Clinton arrived, tens of thousands of protesters poured into the streets carrying <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2012/01/17/bulgaria-chevron-fracking_n_1210299.html" target="_blank">placards</a> that read &quot;Stop fracking with our water&quot; and &quot;Chevron go home.&quot; Bulgaria's parliament responded by voting overwhelmingly for a fracking moratorium.</p>
</div><div id="node-body-break"><div class="billboard extended"><div class="advertise-top norelated"></div><script>ad_code("middleros", 170);</script><p class="post-continues post-continued-from-above norelated"><em><a href="/about/advertising/contact-form">Advertise on MotherJones.com</a></em></p></div></div><div id="node-body-bottom"><p>Clinton urged Bulgarian officials to give fracking another chance. According to Borissov, she <a href="http://www.state.gov/secretary/20092013clinton/rm/2012/02/183342.htm" target="_blank">agreed</a> to help fly in the &quot;best specialists on these new technologies to present the benefits to the Bulgarian people.&quot; But resistance only grew. The following month in neighboring Romania, thousands of people gathered to protest another Chevron fracking project, and Romania's parliament began weighing its own shale gas moratorium. Again Clinton intervened, dispatching her special envoy for energy in Eurasia, Richard Morningstar, to push back against the fracking bans. The State Depart&shy;ment's lobbying effort culminated in late May 2012, when Morningstar held a series of meetings on fracking with top Bulgarian and Romanian officials. He also touted the technology in an interview on Bulgarian national radio, saying it could lead to a fivefold drop in the price of natural gas. A few weeks later, Romania's parliament voted down its proposed fracking ban and Bulgaria's eased its moratorium.</p>

<p>The episode sheds light on a crucial but little-known dimension of Clinton's diplomatic legacy. Under her leadership, the State Department worked closely with energy companies to spread fracking around the globe&mdash;part of a broader push to fight climate change, boost global energy supply, and undercut the power of adversaries such as Russia that use their energy resources as a cudgel. But environmental groups fear that exporting fracking, which has been linked to <a href="http://www.motherjones.com/environment/2013/10/radioactivity-fracking-waste-pennsylvania" target="_blank">drinking-water contamination</a> and <a href="http://www.motherjones.com/environment/2013/03/does-fracking-cause-earthquakes-wastewater-dewatering" target="_blank">earthquakes</a> at home, could wreak havoc in countries with scant environmental regulation. And according to interviews, diplomatic cables, and other documents obtained by <em>Mother Jones</em>, American officials&mdash;some with deep ties to industry&mdash;also helped US firms clinch potentially lucrative shale concessions overseas, raising troubling questions about whose interests the program actually serves.</p>

<div class="inline inline-center" style="display: table; width: 1%"><img alt="Hillary Clinton" class="image" src="/files/clinton_bulgaria_1_630_0.jpg" />
	<div class="caption"><strong>Hillary Clinton is welcomed to Sofia by Bulgarian Foreign Affairs Minister Nikolay Mladenov, left. </strong><a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/statephotos/6851782501/in/set-72157629258024563/">US Department of State</a>/flickr</div>
</div>

<p><br />
	<span class="section-lead">Geologists have long known</span> that there were huge quantities of natural gas locked in shale rock. But tapping it wasn't economically viable until the late 1990s, when a Texas wildcatter named <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/news/the-lives-they-lived/2013/12/21/george-mitchell/" target="_blank">George Mitchell</a> hit on a novel extraction method that involved drilling wells sideways from the initial borehole, then blasting them full of water, chemicals, and sand to break up the shale&mdash;a variation of a technique known as hydraulic fracturing, or fracking. Besides dislodging a bounty of natural gas, Mitchell's breakthrough ignited an energy revolution. Between 2006 and 2008, domestic gas reserves jumped <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2009/06/18/business/energy-environment/18gas.html?_r=0" target="_blank">35 percent</a>. The United States later vaulted past Russia to become the world's largest natural gas producer. As a result, prices dropped to record lows, and America began to wean itself from coal, along with oil and gas imports, which lessened its dependence on the Middle East. The surging global gas supply also helped shrink Russia's economic clout: Profits for Russia's state-owned gas company, Gazprom, plummeted by more than 60 percent between 2008 and 2009 alone.</p>

<p>Clinton, who was sworn in as secretary of state in early 2009, believed that shale gas could help rewrite global energy politics. &quot;This is a moment of profound change,&quot; she later <a href="http://translations.state.gov/st/english/texttrans/2012/10/20121018137692.html" target="_blank">told a crowd</a> at Georgetown University. &quot;Countries that used to depend on others for their energy are now producers. How will this shape world events? Who will benefit, and who will not?&hellip;The answers to these questions are being written right now, and we intend to play a major role.&quot; Clinton tapped a lawyer named David Goldwyn as her special envoy for international energy affairs; <a href="http://www.state.gov/s/ciea/rmk/130461.htm" target="_blank">his charge</a> was &quot;to elevate energy diplomacy as a key function of US foreign policy.&quot;</p>

<div class="pullquote-right">&quot;Countries that used to depend on others for their energy are now producers,&quot; said Clinton. &quot;How will this shape world events? Who will benefit?&hellip;The answers to these questions are being written right now, and we intend to play a major role.&quot;</div>

<p>Goldwyn had a long history of promoting drilling overseas&mdash;both as a Department of Energy official under Bill Clinton and as a representative of the oil industry. From 2005 to 2009 he directed the US-Libya Business Association, an organization funded primarily by US oil companies&mdash;including Chevron, Exxon Mobil, and Marathon&mdash;clamoring to tap Libya's abundant supply. Goldwyn lobbied Congress for pro-Libyan policies and even battled legislation that would have allowed families of the Lockerbie bombing victims to sue the Libyan government for its alleged role in the attack.</p>

<p>According to <a href="https://wikileaks.org/plusd/cables/09STATE111742_a.html" target="_blank">diplomatic cables</a> released by WikiLeaks, one of Goldwyn's first acts at the State Department was gathering oil and gas industry executives &quot;to discuss the potential international impact of shale gas.&quot; Clinton then sent a cable to US diplomats, asking them to collect information on the potential for fracking in their host countries. These efforts eventually gave rise to the <a href="http://www.state.gov/s/ciea/rmk/146249.htm" target="_blank">Global Shale Gas Initiative</a>, which aimed to help other nations develop their shale potential. Clinton <a href="http://www.state.gov/secretary/20092013clinton/rm/2010/04/140286.htm" target="_blank">promised</a> it would do so &quot;in a way that is as environmentally respectful as possible.&quot;</p>

<p>But environmental groups were barely consulted, while industry played a crucial role. When Goldwyn unveiled the initiative in April 2010, it was at a meeting of the United States Energy Association, a trade organization representing Chevron, Exxon Mobil, and ConocoPhillips, all of which were pursuing fracking overseas. Among their top targets was Poland, which preliminary studies suggested had abundant shale gas. The day after Goldwyn's announcement, the US Embassy in Warsaw helped organize a shale gas conference, underwritten by these same companies (plus the oil field services company Halliburton) and attended by officials from the departments of State and Energy.</p>

<p>In some cases, Clinton personally promoted shale gas. During a 2010 gathering of foreign ministers in Washington, DC, she spoke about America's plans to help spread fracking abroad. &quot;I know that in some places [it] is controversial,&quot; <a href="http://www.state.gov/secretary/20092013clinton/rm/2010/04/140286.htm" target="_blank">she said</a>, &quot;but natural gas is the cleanest fossil fuel available for power generation today.&quot; She later traveled to Poland for a series of meetings with officials, after which she announced that the country had joined the Global Shale Gas Initiative.</p>

<div class="pullquote-left">&quot;We are very firm on this,&quot; insists the State Department's Paul Hueper. &quot;We do not shill for industry.&quot;</div>

<p>That August, delegates from 17 countries descended on Washington for the State Depart&shy;ment's first shale gas conference. The media was barred from attending, and officials refused to reveal basic information, including which countries took part. When Rep. Henry Waxman (D-Calif.) inquired about industry involvement, the department would say only that there had been &quot;a limited industry presence.&quot; (State Department officials have since been more forthcoming with <em>Mother Jones</em>: In addition to a number of US government agencies, they say attendees heard from energy firms, including Devon, Chesapeake, and Halliburton.)</p>

<p>During the cursory <a href="http://www.state.gov/s/ciea/rmk/146249.htm" target="_blank">press conference</a> that followed, Goldwyn, a short, bespectacled man with a shock of dark hair, argued that other nations could avoid the environmental damage sometimes associated with fracking by following America's lead and adopting &quot;an umbrella of laws and regulations.&quot; A reporter suggested that US production had actually &quot;outpaced the ability to effectively oversee the safety&quot; and asked how we could be sure the same wouldn't happen elsewhere. Goldwyn replied that attendees had heard about safety issues from energy companies and the Groundwater Protection Council, a nonprofit organization that receives <a href="http://www.motherjones.com/documents/1237898-american-petroleum-institute-990-form#document/p50/a169292" target="_blank">industry funding</a> and opposes federal regulation of fracking wastewater disposal.</p>

<p>Goldwyn and the delegates then boarded a bus to Pennsylvania for an industry-sponsored luncheon and tour of some shale fields. Paul Hueper, director of energy programs at the State Department's Bureau of Energy Resources, says the tour was organized independently and that energy firms were only invited to the conference itself to share best practices. &quot;We are very firm on this,&quot; he insisted. &quot;We do not shill for industry.&quot;<br />
	&nbsp;</p>

<p><span class="section-lead">While the meeting </span>helped stir up interest, it wasn't until 2011 that global fracking fever set in for real. That spring, the US Energy Information Administration (EIA) released its initial estimate of global shale gas, which <a href="http://www.eia.gov/analysis/studies/worldshalegas/" target="_blank">found</a> that 32 countries had viable shale basins and put global recoverable shale gas at 6,600 trillion cubic feet&mdash;enough to supply the world for more than 50 years at current rates of consumption. This was a rich opportunity for big oil and gas companies, which had largely missed out on the US fracking boom and were under pressure from Wall Street to shore up their dwindling reserves. &quot;They're desperate,&quot; says Antoine Simon, who coordinates the shale gas campaign at Friends of the Earth Europe. &quot;It's the last push to continue their fossil fuel development.&quot;</p>

<div class="inline inline-right" style="display: table; width: 1%"><img alt="" class="image" src="/files/gas_lands.gif" /></div>

<p>The industry began fighting hard for access to shale fields abroad, and promoting gas as the fuel of choice for slashing carbon emissions. In Europe, lobbyists circulated a <a href="http://www.motherjones.com/documents/1237926-making-the-green-journey-work" target="_blank">report</a> claiming that the European Union could save 900 billion euros if it invested in gas rather than renewable energy to meet its 2050 climate targets. This rankled environmentalists, <a href="http://www.motherjones.com/environment/2014/09/methane-fracking-obama-climate-change-bill-mckibben" target="_blank">who argue</a> fracking may do little to ease global warming, given that wells and pipelines <a href="http://www.motherjones.com/environment/2014/08/methane-vs-carbon-dioxide-raymond-pierrehumbert-inquiring-minds" target="_blank">leak large quantities of methane</a>, a potent greenhouse gas. They also fear it could crowd out investment in renewables.</p>

<p>By early 2011, the State Department was laying plans to launch a new bureau to integrate energy into every aspect of foreign policy&mdash;an idea Goldwyn had long been advocating. In 2005, he and a Chevron executive named Jan Kalicki had published a book called <a href="http://www.wilsoncenter.org/book/energy-and-security-toward-new-foreign-policy-strategy" target="_blank">Energy and Security: Toward a New Foreign Policy Strategy</a>, which argued that energy independence was unattainable in the near term and urged Washington to shift its focus to energy security&mdash;by boosting global fossil fuel production and stifling unrest that might upset energy markets. Goldwyn and his ideas had played a key role in shaping the bureau, so some observers were surprised when he quietly stepped down just before its launch.</p>

<p>When I approached Goldwyn following a recent speaking engagement in Washington, DC, to ask about his time at the State Department and why he left, he ducked out a side door, and Kalicki blocked the corridor to keep me from following. Goldwyn later said via email that he had simply chosen &quot;to return to the private sector.&quot;</p>

<p>Around the time of his departure, WikiLeaks released a slew of diplomatic cables, including one describing a 2009 meeting during which Goldwyn and Canadian officials discussed development of the Alberta oil sands&mdash;a project benefiting some of the same firms behind the US-Libya Business Association. The <a href="https://wikileaks.org/cable/2009/10/09OTTAWA771.html" target="_blank">cable</a> said that Goldwyn had coached his Canadian counterparts on improving &quot;oil sands messaging&quot; and helped alleviate their concerns about getting oil sands crude to US markets. This embarrassed the State Department, which is reviewing the controversial Keystone XL pipeline proposal to transport crude oil from Canada and is under fire from environmentalists.</p>

<p>After leaving State, Goldwyn took a job with Sutherland, a law and lobbying firm that touts his &quot;deep understanding&quot; of pipeline issues, and launched his own company, Goldwyn Global Strategies.</p>

<p>In late 2011, Clinton finally unveiled the new <a href="http://www.state.gov/e/enr/" target="_blank">Bureau of Energy Resources</a>, with 63 employees and a multimillion-dollar budget. She also <a href="http://translations.state.gov/st/english/texttrans/2012/10/20121018137692.html" target="_blank">promised</a> to instruct US embassies around the globe to step up their work on energy issues and &quot;pursue more outreach to private-sector energy&quot; firms, some of which had generously supported both her and President Barack Obama's political campaigns. (One Chevron executive bundled large sums for Clinton's 2008 presidential bid, for example.)</p>
