                                <p>WILL AMERICA live up to its commitments under the recent climate negotiations in Paris? Its an open question  and an early test will come in Virginia.</p><p>By spring, Gov. Terry McAuliffe will make a decision with huge implications for Hampton Roads and the nation. McAuliffe can side with the states biggest polluter  Dominion Resources  and decide to increase total carbon pollution from power plants. Or McAuliffe can side with President Barack Obama and the spirit of the Paris climate talks to reduce pollution at the state level under the Clean Power Plan.</p><p>Obama told 196 nations last December not to worry about Americas gridlocked Congress. States, he said, will hasten the switch to carbon-free energy. But now its time to deliver. Virginia  a border state, a swing state  could set the national tone.</p><p>Heres the bad news: McAuliffes two-year record on energy has been surprisingly dismal. He has talked a good game on climate change. He made it a big campaign issue when running against former Attorney General  and climate denier  Ken Cuccinelli in 2013.</p><p>But when pressured by big-money fossil fuel interests, McAuliffe has been consistent, siding with polluters almost every time. He supports offshore drilling, even as the City Council in Virginia Beach retreats. Hes been cheerleader-in-chief in supporting fracked-gas pipelines and new power plants. (The associated methane leakage makes those plants nearly as bad as coal.)</p><p>McAuliffe even lobbied the White House and the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency to lower Virginias targets for reducing carbon pollution under the Clean Power Plan. McAuliffe said the original number was too ambitious. Dominion and the coal companies were saying the same thing.</p><p>In August, the EPA announced that Virginias target had been lowered. Up to 1,500 more pollution-related asthma cases could now happen in Virginia by 2030 and dozens more heart attacks.</p>

<p>McAuliffe can redeem himself with a decision on how to implement the CPP. Dominion has found a feature in the CPP that would actually allow Virginia to <em>increase</em> greenhouse gas emissions from power plants by 2030 while still conforming to this federal rule designed to <em>lower</em> such gases. Dominion is lobbying McAuliffes staff to embrace the companys approach.</p><p>Dominion is not the surprise here. It has a national reputation as a corporate steamroller, always pushing for more fossil fuel combustion (and wildly expensive nuclear power) while ignoring or minimizing climate science. The company built a massive new coal-fired power plant in Virginia in 2012 despite warnings from scientists, many shareholders, and some Wall Street analysts that coal was a big risk. Hmmmm.</p><p>Now Dominion CEO Thomas Farrell is pushing for fracked gas use while skipping over the science. In a speech last month sponsored by the Virginia Chamber of Commerce, he acknowledged that clean energy had made strides. But the truth is, we are still in the age of fossil fuels, he stressed, asserting that about 70 percent of U.S. energy now and for the next several decades will come from fossil fuels. Not exactly the spirit of Paris.</p><p>Whats surprising here is that McAuliffe, his poor energy record notwithstanding, would actually consider a state global warming plan that makes global warming worse. If a high-profile Democratic governor can do this, then what real hope is there that America will truly stand up in time to make the dramatic carbon cuts agreed to by 196 countries in Paris?</p><p>Obama should give McAuliffe some advice: Do the right thing. Virginias plan should cover old and new power plants and use a so-called mass-based approach to reducing pollution, not the work-around rate-based approach Dominion prefers.</p><p>McAuliffe gets the final say here. He alone, under the federal rule, gets to craft Virginias pollution plan. Congress has no say, nor does Virginias Republican-controlled General Assembly. And Dominion, despite its lobbying money, doesnt get to decide. Just McAuliffe.</p><p>Sources say the governor will make up his mind by late spring. In all fairness to Virginia voters and to supporters of the Paris agreement worldwide, he should immediately announce his support of a Virginia pollution plan that reduces total carbon emissions, thus meeting the planets needs, if not Dominions.</p>
